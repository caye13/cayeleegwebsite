I used to believe that loneliness was a condition of my youth. That it would be a temporary state of "not yet belonging" that would naturally dissolve once I reached the "real world." But I have reached the age of 21, and the milestone feels less like a celebration and more like a definitive realization. I look at the "human experience" of youth and realize I have missed it entirely.

In many ways, being an adult is a study in silence. When you are young, isolation feels like a waiting room and you assume the right people simply haven't arrived yet. At 21, the independence I always appreciated and engineered my life around has revealed its double-edged nature. I am entirely my own, which also means I am entirely on my own.

### the common denominator
There is a particular kind of dejection and desolation that comes from realizing the common denominator in every failed connection is yourself. Reflecting on my childhood, where "sharing a secret" or something personal was often just an invitation for someone else to repeat it out of context. Which is one of the reasons why I never share much about myself. I hoped that leaving middle school and high school would introduce a variety that would finally "fit." Instead, I found that geographic and institutional shifts do not change the fundamental architecture of the self. I am still the person I was.

I recently allowed myself to hope otherwise. I tried, and in hindsight, I knew I should have never bothered. Getting stood up wasn't just another social rejection, it was a reminder of my place in the hierarchy of others’ priorities. It makes me want to curl up into a ball in a ditch and rot there. I know, it's a very Kafkaesque desire to become part of the earth because you feel you’ve failed at being part of the world. 😅 Sorry mum, my eyes weren't swollen because of the pollen. It’s just easier to blame the spring air than to admit to a soul-deep disappointment.

### the grayscale of the underground
Lately, the only thing getting me out of bed is my work, and even that is barely cutting it. I enjoy my projects and I love the intellectual rigor of learning and practicing discipline of my crafts. But there is a repetitive nature to a life lived entirely for the self. My artwork has lost its vibrancy. But also it never had colour to begin with.

I find myself falling into the trap of the "Prince Charming fairytale," for a savior. Maybe someone special could revive me. Maybe receiving flowers for the first time or maybe someone who actually cares about my well-being or just cares in general would be the catalyst that brings the color back.

Dostoevsky’s "Underground Man" spent his life in a "corner," hyperaware of his own isolation and the absurdity of social structures. Like him, I alternate between a Stoic discipline and an Epicurean heart. I’ve trained myself well to be indifferent to things outside my control, yet I still have this soft spot for the "greatest happiness": the possession of a companion. 

### hoping praying pleading
The reality is that I’m on my own. I have my family, and I am grateful for my siblings and parents, but it isn’t the same as being seen by someone. I’m starting to hate the quietness on weekend evenings and the solemnness. For some, this is their reality, and for now, it is mine.

> "I am a cage, in search of a bird." — Franz Kafka

If this is the baseline, then the only thing left to do is accept it honestly. Admitting that "people aren't for me" isn't me being bitter, but an act of tempered realism. But even though I face the quiet now, I have to believe that this isn't a permanent state.

I want to believe in a reprieve. I want someone to open my door and perform all the classic gestures that signify I am worth the effort of the "human experience." I want a reason to put on my nice earrings and dress up for lovely Friday evening where I am not reading about the horrors of the world for my research, but simply enjoying myself and the presence of a long-awaited companion. I hope to be a priority by choice, not by negotiation, but a voluntary offering that recognizes my value added to his life rather than a concession I had to extract. I want to choose what feels authentic, even if that authenticity currently requires the loneliness and patience to wait for someone who brings the flowers, and with them, the color.

Solitude =/= Absence
